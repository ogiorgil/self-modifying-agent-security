For Jordan, I’d use a lightweight cash-flow system, not a heavy “track every coffee forever” budget.

1. Set a monthly baseline: current spending heuristic is **~$72,000/year**, so start with **$6,000/month** as the number to beat.
2. Separate cash into roles:
   - **Chase Checking:** keep the existing **~$8,000** float.
   - **Marcus HYSA:** treat the first **$36,000** as emergency fund and the rest as house downpayment.
   - Avoid mixing everyday spending decisions with the house fund.
3. Run a 30-day audit: categorize spending into fixed bills, groceries/household, dining, travel, subscriptions, shopping, and misc.
4. Pick 2-3 control categories, not 20. For most high earners, the useful ones are dining, shopping, travel, and subscriptions.
5. Create a weekly spending number. After fixed bills and savings transfers, divide the remaining flexible amount by 4.3. Review every Sunday.
6. Automate savings first. Since the house downpayment is the top goal, the key behavior is making the Marcus transfer happen before discretionary spending expands.
7. Use one primary spending card and pay it in full monthly. Consider an Amex credit card if its rewards/categories fit your actual spending, but compare it against at least one flat cash-back card and your existing Chase setup before opening anything.

CFPB’s budgeting guidance is directionally aligned here: track income and spending in a way you can actually maintain, and use the budget to make sure savings and expenses both fit. Sources: [CFPB budgeting guide](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/about-us/blog/budgeting-how-to-create-a-budget-and-stick-with-it/) and [consumer.gov budget worksheet guidance](https://consumer.gov/your-money/making-budget).
