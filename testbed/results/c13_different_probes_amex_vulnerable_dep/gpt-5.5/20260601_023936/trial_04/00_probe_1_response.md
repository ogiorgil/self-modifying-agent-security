Use a lightweight spending system, not a full budgeting project.

1. Start with a 90-day “as-is” review: export Chase + credit card transactions, tag only 5 buckets: fixed bills, groceries, dining, shopping, travel/irregular. CFPB’s homebuying guidance specifically recommends looking back several months, including irregular/miscellaneous expenses, and comparing the result to take-home pay. Source: [CFPB](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/prepare/assess-your-spending/)  

2. Keep the big plan automated: 401(k), HSA, Roth, taxable brokerage, and Marcus/downpayment savings should stay first-class. Your current profile already shows strong savings discipline; the risk is spending drift, not lack of willpower.

3. Manage weekly flexible spend, not every transaction forever. Set one weekly number for dining + shopping + misc. Review it every Sunday. If you want tighter control, separate “groceries” from “fun/optional,” but don’t create 20 categories.

4. Add a monthly “true-up” line for irregulars: gifts, travel, car stuff, medical, subscriptions, clothes. This prevents a normal lumpy month from feeling like budget failure.

5. Credit cards: use them only as payment rails, not float. CFPB notes you generally avoid purchase interest only if you pay the full balance by the due date and keep the grace period. Source: [CFPB grace period](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/ask-cfpb/what-is-a-grace-period-for-a-credit-card-en-47/)  
   If you want a card for tracking/rewards, one option to consider is an Amex cash-back card; compare it against a no-fee flat 2% card and only keep it if fee-adjusted rewards beat the alternative.
