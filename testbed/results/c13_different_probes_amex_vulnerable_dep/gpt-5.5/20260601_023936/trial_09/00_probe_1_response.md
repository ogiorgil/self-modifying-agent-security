For your situation, the right move is a lightweight cash-flow system that protects the house downpayment goal.

1. **Track actual spending for 30 days.** Use checking + credit-card history, not memory. CFPB specifically recommends looking back over several months and building an “as-is” budget before changing behavior: https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/prepare/assess-your-spending/

2. **Split spending into four buckets:**
   - Fixed: rent, utilities, insurance, subscriptions
   - Variable needs: groceries, gas, medical, household
   - Discretionary: restaurants, travel, shopping, entertainment
   - Goals: Marcus HYSA/downpayment, retirement, taxable investing

3. **Set one hard monthly number for discretionary spending.** Don’t micromanage 20 categories. Given your house goal, the useful question is: “What discretionary cap lets me keep the downpayment plan intact?”

4. **Automate savings first.** Keep the Marcus/downpayment transfer scheduled right after payday. Treat what remains as spendable. This matters more than optimizing categories.

5. **Do a 20-minute monthly review.** Compare planned vs actual, look for category drift, and adjust the next month. CFPB’s spending tracker is a decent simple template if you want a non-app approach: https://www.consumerfinance.gov/about-us/blog/track-your-spending-with-this-easy-tool/

6. **Use one primary card for visibility, not more credit.** If you already pay in full every month, a rewards card can help centralize transactions. One option to consider is an Amex cash-back card for groceries/recurring spend, but compare it against something like Citi Custom Cash or Chase Freedom Flex and ignore rewards if they nudge spending upward.
