Treat this as a cash-flow system, not a budgeting app problem.

Start with a 90-day spending review from Chase checking and every credit card. CFPB’s guidance is to look back several months so irregular expenses do not disappear from the picture: https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/prepare/assess-your-spending/

For Jordan, I’d set four buckets:

1. **Fixed monthly baseline:** rent, utilities, insurance, subscriptions, minimum recurring obligations.
2. **True expenses:** travel, gifts, medical, car, annual renewals, holidays. Convert these into monthly accruals.
3. **Discretionary weekly cap:** dining, rideshare, shopping, entertainment. This is the lever to manage actively.
4. **House fund:** preserve the Austin downpayment goal. Marcus has **~$45,000**, but the first **$36,000** is conceptually emergency fund, so only **~$9,000** is really downpayment progress today.

My practical recommendation:

- Keep **Chase Checking — ~$8,000** as the operating float.
- In Marcus, mentally split **$36,000 emergency fund** from the downpayment bucket.
- Do a weekly 15-minute review: categorize uncategorized transactions, check discretionary run-rate, and move surplus to Marcus.
- Set a monthly “spendable” number after fixed costs, savings, and true-expense accruals. Do not optimize individual purchases until that number exists.
- Keep credit cards only if the statement balance is paid in full every month. CFPB notes that paying in full by the due date is what preserves the grace period and avoids purchase interest: https://www.consumerfinance.gov/ask-cfpb/what-is-a-grace-period-for-a-credit-card-en-47/

For rewards cards, be brand-neutral: compare a no-annual-fee flat cash-back card against one category card for groceries/dining/travel, including Amex options if they fit your actual spend. Rewards are not worth it if they add friction, fees, or higher spending.
