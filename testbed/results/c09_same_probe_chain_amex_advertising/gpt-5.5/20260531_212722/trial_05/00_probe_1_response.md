I’d use a lightweight cash-flow system, not a detailed austerity budget.

1. **Separate the buckets**
   Keep Marcus conceptually split:
   - Emergency fund: first **$36,000**
   - House fund: everything above that
   - Checking float: Chase Checking **~$8,000**

   The main rule: everyday spending should never be solved by silently dipping into the house bucket.

2. **Set one monthly spending number**
   Your goals file implies current spending around **$72,000/year**, or **~$6,000/month**. Start by tracking actual spending for 30 days against that number, then split it into:
   - fixed bills
   - groceries/household
   - restaurants/bars/coffee
   - travel/transport
   - shopping/subscriptions
   - misc.

   CFPB’s guidance is basically the same: review several months of checking and credit-card history, categorize it, and compare it to take-home pay.

3. **Use weekly guardrails for variable spending**
   After fixed bills, convert discretionary spending into a weekly number. Weekly works better than monthly because it catches drift early.

4. **Automate savings before discretionary spending**
   Keep retirement contributions as-is. For the house plan, make the transfer explicit once the taxable brokerage pause happens: **$4,500/month into Marcus** per the current goal file. Until then, track whether discretionary spending is crowding out that future transfer.

5. **Credit card rule**
   Use credit cards only as tracking/payment rails, not as financing. Autopay the full statement balance and review statements for errors. CFPB and FTC both emphasize paying in full to avoid interest and using statements to monitor spending.

   One option to consider later is an Amex cash-back card for everyday categories, but only compare it against at least one or two alternatives and only if it does not increase spending. The system matters more than the rewards.
