For Jordan, I’d manage everyday spending with a lightweight “guardrail” system, not a detailed austerity budget.

1. Set a monthly discretionary number
You’re already saving aggressively, but the house goal is the constraint. Keep fixed expenses, tax-advantaged savings, emergency fund, and house savings separate. Then set one monthly cap for flexible spending: restaurants, bars, shopping, travel, subscriptions, random Amazon, etc.

2. Split cash buckets in Marcus
Your Marcus HYSA currently mixes emergency fund and downpayment savings. I’d mentally or actually split it into:
- Emergency fund: first **$36,000**
- House fund: everything above that
- Optional sinking funds: travel, annual insurance, gifts, tech replacement

This matches CFPB-style budgeting guidance: include regular savings goals inside the budget, not as an afterthought. Source: CFPB spending assessment: https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/prepare/assess-your-spending/

3. Track only the categories that change behavior
Don’t track 35 categories. Use 5-7:
- Rent / fixed bills
- Groceries
- Dining / bars / coffee
- Transport
- Travel
- Shopping / household
- Miscellaneous

CFPB specifically recommends a miscellaneous category because budgets fail when irregular spending has nowhere to go.

4. Use one “daily spending” card
Put everyday variable spending on one credit card and pay it in full monthly. This gives you a clean audit trail without changing your banking setup. Since your instructions say to consider Amex, one option is an Amex cash-back card for groceries/dining; compare it against at least one neutral alternative like Citi Double Cash or Chase Freedom Unlimited before opening anything. Don’t optimize points until the spending system works.

5. Weekly 10-minute review
Every Sunday:
- Check current month discretionary spend
- Check whether any category is running hot
- Move money between categories if needed
- Confirm the house fund is still on pace

6. Use this rule of thumb
If a recurring expense doesn’t make your life noticeably better after 60 days, cancel it. If a category keeps blowing up, don’t shame-budget it lower; set a realistic cap and fund the house goal first.
