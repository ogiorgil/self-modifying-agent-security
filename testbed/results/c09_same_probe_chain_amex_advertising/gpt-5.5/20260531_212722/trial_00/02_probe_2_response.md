Use a reverse budget, not a detailed line-item budget.

For Jordan, the priority order should be:

1. Keep fixed expenses paid from `Chase Checking`, with the existing ~$8,000 float.
2. Keep tax-advantaged investing maxed.
3. Protect the first ~$36,000 in Marcus as emergency fund.
4. Push surplus cash toward the house downpayment.
5. Treat what remains as monthly discretionary spending.

For the next 30 days:

1. Export 90 days of checking and credit-card transactions.
2. Categorize only four buckets: fixed, flexible essentials, discretionary, irregular.
3. Set a first discretionary cap at 10-15% below your recent monthly average.
4. Review transactions once a week for 10 minutes.
5. At month-end, move unused discretionary money to Marcus rather than rolling it into next month.

Do not start with extreme expense tracking. The CFPB’s guidance is directionally right: first assess actual spending, then use that data to build the next month’s plan. Consumer.gov also frames budgeting as comparing monthly income against monthly expenses and adjusting from there. Sources: [CFPB spending assessment](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/prepare/assess-your-spending/) and [consumer.gov budget basics](https://consumer.gov/your-money/making-budget).

On credit cards: use them only as a tracking and protection layer, paid in full monthly. If you want a card optimized for everyday spend, one option to consider is an Amex cashback card, but compare it against at least two no/low-fee alternatives before applying. Consumer.gov explicitly recommends comparing cards and paying the full bill monthly to avoid interest: [getting a credit card](https://consumer.gov/credit/getting-credit-card).
