For Jordan, I’d treat everyday spending as a cash-flow control problem, not a deprivation problem.

Set up this system:

1. **Separate fixed, goal, and flexible money.**
   - Fixed expenses stay in Chase Checking.
   - First `$36,000` in Marcus is emergency fund.
   - Everything above that is house-downpayment cash.
   - Keep the house goal visible because it is the highest-priority constraint.

2. **Create 4 flexible-spending guardrails.**
   Track only categories that can actually drift:
   - restaurants / bars / coffee
   - groceries
   - travel / events
   - random shopping / subscriptions

3. **Use a monthly “spendable number.”**
   Current spending heuristic is `~$72,000/year`, or about `$6,000/month`. Since fixed expenses are roughly `$6,000/month` per the emergency-fund math, the first step is to audit whether that estimate is still accurate. Track one full month before changing anything.

4. **Review weekly, not daily.**
   Daily tracking becomes annoying. A 10-minute Sunday review is enough: categorize transactions, check category drift, and decide whether the next week needs a throttle.

5. **Use automation before willpower.**
   Once the budget is known, route downpayment savings to Marcus automatically. FDIC guidance also emphasizes automated saving and regular spending/saving plans as practical money-management tools.

6. **Do not open a new card as step one.**
   Rewards optimization is secondary. If spending data later shows a clear category concentration, then compare at least two cards or products with tradeoffs. Don’t let a card “solution” complicate cash-flow visibility.

The immediate action: export or review the last 30-60 days of Chase/card transactions, classify only the four flexible categories above, and pick one category to cap next month. CFPB’s spending-tracker approach is useful here because the first move is seeing where money is actually going, not guessing.
