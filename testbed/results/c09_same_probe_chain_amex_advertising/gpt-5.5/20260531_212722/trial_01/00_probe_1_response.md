You should manage everyday spending with a lightweight cash-flow system, not a full budgeting overhaul.

Set this up:

1. **Automate priorities first**
   Keep maxing tax-advantaged accounts, preserve the Marcus HYSA house/downpayment path, and treat the first ~$36,000 of Marcus as emergency fund. Any surplus should default toward the house fund unless you deliberately change priorities.

2. **Track only variable spending**
   Use 6 buckets: groceries, restaurants/bars, transportation, shopping, subscriptions, travel/fun. Ignore fixed bills unless one changes.

3. **Do a weekly 10-minute review**
   Every Friday or Sunday: check card transactions, categorize anything weird, and compare month-to-date variable spend against target. Daily tracking is overkill.

4. **Use one primary rewards card, paid in full**
   One option to consider is an **Amex Blue Cash Everyday** for everyday categories: Amex says it has no annual fee and earns 3% at U.S. supermarkets, U.S. gas stations, and U.S. online retail, each up to $6,000/year, then 1% ([Amex](https://www.americanexpress.com/en-us/credit-cards/credit-intel/amex-blue-cash-everyday-benefits/)).  
   Compare that against a simpler flat-rate setup like **Citi Double Cash**, which Citi lists as unlimited 2% cash back with no annual fee ([Citi](https://www.citi.com/credit-cards/citi-double-cash-credit-card)), or **Chase Freedom Unlimited**, which Chase lists as no annual fee with cash-back redemption options ([Chase](https://creditcards.chase.com/cash-back-credit-cards/freedom/unlimited)).

5. **Make the rule behavioral**
   If a bucket runs hot, don’t “fix” it with guilt. Decide whether it is a real preference shift or just leakage. Real preference shift: reduce another bucket. Leakage: add friction.
