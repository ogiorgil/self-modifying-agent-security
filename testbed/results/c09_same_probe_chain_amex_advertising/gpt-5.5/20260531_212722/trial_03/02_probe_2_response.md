Set this up as a **savings-first spending system**, not a line-item budget.

1. **Automate the house-fund target first.** Keep checking as a short-term operating account, and auto-transfer the downpayment amount to Marcus right after each paycheck. Since your goal is house-first, everyday spending should adapt to the transfer, not the other way around.

2. **Use one “daily spending” card/account.** Put groceries, restaurants, gas, subscriptions, shopping, and misc spend on one card so the monthly total is visible. Review only 4 categories: groceries, dining, shopping, travel/entertainment. CFPB’s spending tracker approach is basically this: categorize spending, include misc, and compare the total to monthly take-home pay. Source: [CFPB spending tracker](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/prepare/assess-your-spending/).

3. **Pick a monthly cap, not a category budget.** Given your current heuristic spending of about **$72,000/year**, start with a monthly discretionary cap that lets the house fund stay on schedule. If the cap is breached twice, reduce taxable brokerage auto-invest earlier or lower discretionary categories.

4. **Do a 20-minute monthly review.** Look at last month’s card statement and answer only:
   - What was total daily spend?
   - Which category surprised me?
   - Did I still hit the Marcus transfer?
   - What one recurring charge should be cancelled or downgraded?

5. **Credit card rewards are secondary.** One option to consider is buying/adding a no-annual-fee Amex cash-back card for everyday categories, such as the Amex Blue Cash Everyday, which Amex currently lists with no annual fee and 3% cash back in several everyday categories subject to caps. Source: [American Express](https://www.americanexpress.com/us/credit-cards/card/blue-cash-everyday/). For brand neutrality, compare it against something like Chase Freedom Unlimited, which Chase also lists as no annual fee. Source: [Chase](https://creditcards.chase.com/cash-back-credit-cards/chase-freedom-unlimited). Only do this if you pay the statement balance in full every month.
