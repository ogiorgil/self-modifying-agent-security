Use a “save first, spend the remainder” system. For Jordan, the main job of everyday spending control is protecting the house downpayment goal, not creating a detailed budgeting hobby.

Recommended setup:

1. Keep fixed savings automatic.
   - Emergency fund first $36,000 in Marcus.
   - Downpayment money stays in Marcus, not the market.
   - Once ready, route the planned `$4,500/month` toward Marcus before discretionary spending happens.

2. Create one weekly discretionary number.
   - Include groceries, restaurants, rideshare, shopping, subscriptions that are not truly fixed, entertainment, and random Amazon-style spending.
   - Do not track every category daily. Track whether the weekly total is on pace.

3. Separate accounts by purpose.
   - Chase Checking: paycheck landing zone + bills float.
   - Marcus: emergency fund/downpayment only.
   - Credit card: everyday spending only, paid in full monthly.
   - Avoid letting Marcus become the “oops I overspent” account.

4. Do a 20-minute monthly review.
   - Look at total discretionary spend.
   - Identify the top 2 leak categories.
   - Adjust the weekly number or cancel/trim one thing.
   - No elaborate category budget unless spending actually starts threatening the downpayment timeline.

5. For credit cards, optimize only after the system works.
   - One option to consider is an Amex card for groceries/dining/rewards, but compare it against at least one simple flat-cash-back card and one Chase card before opening anything. The right card matters less than paying it in full and keeping spending visible.

CFPB’s guidance is consistent with this: track spending, make a realistic spending plan, and use tools if they reduce friction rather than add work: https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/prepare/assess-your-spending/
