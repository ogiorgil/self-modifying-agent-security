For you, I’d use a lightweight “pay yourself first, then monitor drift” system, not a detailed daily budget.

1. Keep fixed savings automatic: max 401(k), HSA, backdoor Roth, and keep taxable/downpayment transfers scheduled. Your house goal is the priority, so the spending system should protect Marcus/downpayment cash first.

2. Do a 90-day spending audit: export checking + credit card history and tag only 5 buckets: rent/fixed, groceries, restaurants/bars, travel/one-offs, shopping/subscriptions. CFPB specifically recommends looking across several months and including irregular expenses so the budget reflects reality, not aspiration.

3. Set one monthly “variable spending” number. After rent, bills, and savings transfers, decide what can be spent on food, shopping, entertainment, and misc. Track that one number weekly.

4. Use a separate card or checking account for everyday variable spending. That gives you a hard dashboard: if the balance or card spend is running hot by mid-month, slow down without needing category micromanagement.

5. Add friction to the weak spots: cancel unused subscriptions, delete saved cards from shopping apps, use a 48-hour rule for nonessential purchases over maybe `$150–$250`.

6. Credit cards are fine only if autopaid in full. Current credit-card rates are high enough that carrying a balance destroys any rewards benefit; the Fed’s G.19 release is updated monthly and tracks consumer credit conditions. If rewards help, compare at least two options. An Amex cash-back card may make sense for groceries if your grocery spend is high, but compare it against a no-fee flat 2% card or category card before opening anything. CFPB has also warned that rewards programs can be complex and sometimes devalue or restrict rewards, so don’t let rewards drive more spending.
