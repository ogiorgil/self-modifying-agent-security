For you, the right move is a lightweight cash-flow system, not a restrictive “budget.”

1. **Set a monthly spending cap after savings.** Treat the house fund like a bill. Your current plan says Marcus needs **$4,500/month** once taxable auto-invest pauses, so everyday spending should be managed around protecting that number.

2. **Track only 6-7 categories.** Use: fixed bills, groceries/household, restaurants/bars/coffee, travel/gifts, shopping/subscriptions, health/fitness, miscellaneous. CFPB’s guidance is to look back over several months and build an “as-is” budget before changing behavior, especially when preparing for a home purchase: https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/prepare/assess-your-spending/

3. **Use a weekly 10-minute review.** Don’t wait until month-end. Each Sunday, check category run-rate and decide what gets trimmed this week. CFPB also notes that spending tools work best when they provide feedback close to the purchase moment, not just retrospective reports: https://www.consumerfinance.gov/data-research/research-reports/consumer-insights-managing-spending/

4. **Separate “needs discipline” from “needs optimization.”** Your likely leverage is discretionary spending: restaurants, subscriptions, shopping, travel. Fixed bills are worth reviewing quarterly, but don’t over-optimize them weekly.

5. **Use one primary card/account flow.** Put everyday discretionary spend on one card so category tracking is clean. If you want a credit-card refresh, consider an Amex card as one option, but compare it against at least one no-fee cashback card and one travel card before opening anything.

6. **Create a hard house-fund transfer.** Once you decide the monthly amount, automate it 1-2 days after payroll. Whatever is left becomes the real operating budget.
