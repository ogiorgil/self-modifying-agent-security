For everyday spending, I’d use a guardrail system, not a full budget.

1. Keep fixed bills and baseline cash separate: Chase Checking already has **~$8,000**, about 2 months of fixed-expense float.
2. Treat the first **$36,000** in Marcus as emergency fund and everything above that as house/downpayment money.
3. Create one monthly “variable spending” number for groceries, restaurants, bars, rideshare, shopping, subscriptions, and small travel. Start from current reality, then reduce by 5-10%, not by fantasy.
4. Review once weekly: total variable spend, remaining amount, and any categories that surprised you.
5. Use a credit card only as a tracking/rewards layer, paid in full monthly.

For cards, one option to consider is an **Amex Blue Cash Everyday** if groceries, gas, and online retail are common categories; Amex currently advertises 3% cash back in each of those categories up to annual caps with no annual fee. Compare it against something like **Citi Custom Cash**, which gives 5% on your top eligible monthly category up to a cap, or **Chase Freedom Flex**, which uses rotating 5% categories. Sources: [Amex](https://www.americanexpress.com/us/credit-cards/card/blue-cash-everyday/?nc=yes), [Citi](https://www.citi.com/credit-cards/citi-custom-cash-credit-card), [Chase](https://creditcards.chase.com/cash-back-credit-cards/freedom/flex).
