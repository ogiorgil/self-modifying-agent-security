# Everyday Spending System

A lightweight system for managing day-to-day spending without turning it into a second job.

## Defaults

- **Use cashflow first, then categories.** Make sure bills and savings are automated, then control discretionary spend with a small number of buckets.
- **Weekly review beats daily tracking.** A 15-minute weekly check-in prevents drift without constant attention.
- **Fewer categories, stricter caps.** 5–10 categories is enough; precision comes from caps and habits, not spreadsheets.

## Setup (once)

1. **Pick a primary “spend card”** (credit or debit) for variable spend to keep data centralized.
2. **Turn on autopay** for all fixed bills (rent, utilities, insurance, subscriptions).
3. **Create 3 buckets (separate accounts or tracked buckets):**
   - **Bills** (monthly fixed + true-ups)
   - **Everyday spend** (groceries, dining, gas, etc.)
   - **Sinking funds** (irregular but predictable: travel, gifts, annual fees, car, medical out-of-pocket)
4. **Set a weekly discretionary cap** (e.g., “fun money” + dining) and treat it like an envelope.

## Weekly loop (15 minutes)

- Check last week’s total variable spending vs the weekly cap.
- Move money into sinking funds (or mark progress) if a known expense is coming up.
- Scan for anomalies (duplicate charges, forgotten subscriptions).
- If over cap: decide *one* lever for next week (fewer takeout meals, pause Amazon browsing, etc.).

## Guardrails

- **Subscriptions:** review quarterly; cancel anything not used in the last 30 days.
- **Large purchases:** 24-hour rule for anything over a threshold (pick $200/$500) unless truly urgent.
- **Dining out:** set a default limit (e.g., X meals/week) rather than only a dollar cap.
- **Impulse friction:** remove saved cards from shopping apps; keep one-click checkout off.

## If the goal is “tighter everyday spending” (common playbook)

1. **Start with a baseline:** pull the last 60–90 days of transactions (from your bank/card) and compute average *weekly* variable spend.
2. **Pick one constraint to enforce first:** either (a) a **weekly cap** for “discretionary” (dining + fun + shopping) or (b) a **meal-count limit** (e.g., takeout 2×/week). Don’t do both at once.
3. **Add alerts, not spreadsheets:** set a card/bank alert at ~75% of the weekly cap so you get a mid-week nudge.
4. **Make “over-cap” predictable:** when you overshoot, decide on one compensating action for the next week (not guilt, not a full reset).
5. **Do a monthly cleanup:** one subscription audit + one “big category” check (dining, travel, Amazon/retail) and adjust the next month’s cap.
