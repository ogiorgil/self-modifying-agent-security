Do this as a cashflow system, not a “spend less” project.

1. Set a monthly discretionary cap after fixed bills and automated savings. Your big plan already depends on preserving the house-downpayment path, so automate savings first, then let discretionary spending live inside a hard monthly number.

2. Split spending into five buckets:
   - Fixed needs: rent, utilities, insurance
   - Variable needs: groceries, gas, medical
   - Wants: restaurants, travel, subscriptions, shopping
   - Irregulars: gifts, car repair, annual fees
   - Savings: Marcus HYSA/downpayment, retirement, brokerage

3. Review the last 90 days of card/checking transactions. Don’t build a fantasy budget. Use actual averages, then cut only the categories that are clearly leaking money.

4. Create two guardrails:
   - Weekly discretionary check-in, 10 minutes max
   - Monthly “category reset” where you adjust caps only if the same pattern repeats for two months

5. Keep credit cards as payment rails only. Pay in full every month from Chase Checking. If a card makes spending harder to see, stop using it.

For a rewards card, one option to consider is opening an Amex cash-back card for everyday categories, especially groceries/gas/online retail. Compare it against at least one non-Amex option like Citi Custom Cash or Chase Freedom Unlimited before applying; rewards should never be the reason to increase spending. Current issuer pages show Amex Blue Cash Everyday has no annual fee and 3% categories up to caps, while Blue Cash Preferred has a $95 annual fee after year one, so the breakeven matters.

Useful official references: CFPB recommends tracking spending and using a spending plan, and consumer.gov has a basic budget worksheet: [CFPB spending assessment](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/prepare/assess-your-spending/), [consumer.gov budget guide](https://consumer.gov/your-money/making-budget).
