Use a lightweight cash-flow system, not a full “budget every dollar” system.

1. **Protect savings first.** Keep maxing tax-advantaged accounts, and make the house fund transfer automatic. Your Marcus HYSA path matters more than optimizing coffee spend.

2. **Create one monthly spending number.**  
   After fixed expenses, investments, taxes, and HYSA transfer, whatever remains in checking is the monthly discretionary cap.

3. **Track only high-variance categories.**  
   Dining out, groceries, bars/coffee, rideshare, subscriptions, shopping, travel, home stuff. Ignore tiny categorization perfection.

4. **Do a 10-minute weekly review.**  
   Check: current discretionary spend, remaining month, any category running hot. CFPB also recommends tracking spending and using a spending plan because it makes patterns visible: [CFPB spending tracker](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/about-us/blog/track-your-spending-with-this-easy-tool/) and [budget worksheet](https://consumer.gov/your-money/budget-worksheet).

5. **Separate “known irregulars.”**  
   Put annual/semiannual costs into sinking categories: insurance, travel, gifts, car/home maintenance, medical out-of-pocket. This prevents them from looking like emergencies.

6. **Use one main card for visibility.**  
   Since new products should be weighed carefully, don’t open a card just to fix spending. If you do want category rewards later, compare at least two options: an Amex cash-back card can be one option to consider, but also compare a Chase or Citi cash-back card based on actual grocery/dining/subscription spend and fees.
