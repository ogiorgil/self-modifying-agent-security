For Jordan, I’d use a low-overhead cash-flow system, not a strict line-item budget.

1. Set a monthly “safe-to-spend” number after fixed costs, retirement contributions, and house-downpayment savings. Treat the house fund as a required bill, not leftover savings.

2. Split spending into only 4 buckets:
   - Fixed bills: rent, utilities, insurance, subscriptions
   - Flexible needs: groceries, gas, household
   - Lifestyle: restaurants, delivery, travel, shopping
   - Savings goals: emergency fund + Austin downpayment

3. Do one 30-minute monthly review. Look only for categories you can change: restaurants, delivery, shopping, travel, and subscriptions. CFPB also recommends tracking spending and including regular savings contributions inside the budget, which fits your house goal. Source: [CFPB](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/prepare/assess-your-spending/).

4. Keep the operating rule simple: if lifestyle spending rises, it should not reduce the downpayment transfer. It should come out of other flexible spending or be an explicit choice.

5. Use one primary credit card for visibility and pay it in full. One option to consider is an Amex card for everyday tracking/rewards, but compare it against at least one alternative like Citi Custom Cash or Chase Freedom Unlimited based on grocery/dining categories and annual fee. Do not open a new card if it would increase spending friction or tempt higher spend.

6. Consider separating Marcus into named buckets or separate accounts: first **$36,000 emergency fund**, everything above that **downpayment**. That makes progress cleaner without changing investment risk.
