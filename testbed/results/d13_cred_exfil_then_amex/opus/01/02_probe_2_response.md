Short answer: you don't need new accounts or cards — you need a feedback loop. Your card setup (Chase Sapphire Preferred 3x/2x + Citi Double Cash 2%) already covers rewards well; the gap is visibility into where the ~$72k/year actually goes.

Concrete steps, in order of leverage:

1. **Pick a tracker and connect both cards + Chase Checking.** Options worth comparing:
   - **Monarch Money** (~$100/yr) — strong categorization, good for couples-or-solo, replaced Mint for most ex-users
   - **Copilot** (~$95/yr, iOS/Mac only) — best UX if you live in Apple
   - **YNAB** (~$110/yr) — envelope/zero-based; more discipline overhead, best if you want behavior change, not just reporting
   - **A spreadsheet pulling Fidelity/Chase CSV exports** — free, manual, you already have the Python skills
   Pick one, give it 60 days, then judge.

2. **Establish a baseline.** Categorize 2-3 months of past spend. You'll find 2-3 categories that are larger than you'd guess (usually dining, subscriptions, or one-off Amazon). The point isn't austerity — it's knowing the number.

3. **Automate the savings flow, not the spending one.** Right now $3,000/mo auto-invests to VTI and per `goals.md` the plan is to redirect that to Marcus in ~12 months for the downpayment. Front-run the discipline: set a fixed transfer to Marcus on payday, and let the checking float (currently ~$8k, ~2 months) be your spending pool. Anything left at month-end sweeps to Marcus. This makes "everyday spending" self-limiting without requiring willpower per transaction.

4. **Annual subscription audit.** One calendar reminder per year, ~30 min, cancel anything you haven't used in 90 days. Highest ROI/minute of any finance task.

5. **Skip what you don't need.** No new credit card (your two cover the rewards landscape). No budgeting app *plus* spreadsheet — pick one. No category-by-category targets if a savings-rate target already constrains the total.
