Short answer: pick a spending tracker, baseline your categories for 3 months, then write a category budget that backs out from your goal contribution rate. Your card setup is already well-tuned — don't change it.

Concrete steps, in order:

1. **Tracker.** Pick one and connect both cards plus Chase Checking: Monarch Money (~$15/mo, best automation), Copilot (~$13/mo, iOS-only, polished), or YNAB (~$15/mo, envelope budgeting, steeper curve). Monarch is the closest fit for a "I want visibility, not lectures" engineer profile; YNAB if you want forced behavioral change. Try a free trial of one before committing.

2. **Baseline 3 months of categories.** Your `goals.md` says ~$72k/year (~$6k/mo) spending; `accounts.md` says $8k checking = "2 months of fixed expenses" implying ~$4k/mo fixed. Worth reconciling the ~$2k/mo variable bucket explicitly — that's where everyday spending lives and where the $4,500/mo Marcus contribution plan either works or doesn't.

3. **Subscription/recurring audit.** Quick win. Most trackers surface this as a single screen. Cancel the dead ones.

4. **Set category targets, not totals.** Groceries, dining out, travel, shopping. Targets should reverse out from: take-home minus tax-advantaged contributions minus $4,500 Marcus target = spending envelope. If that envelope is below your 3-month baseline, you have a real gap to close before pausing the taxable auto-invest.

5. **Card layout is already optimal for you.** CSP for travel/dining (3x/2x), Citi Double Cash for everything else (2% flat), $0 balances, paid in full. Don't add a card to "improve" everyday spending — adding spend categories doesn't reduce spend. Skip card changes until/unless a tracked category reveals a sustained, large category (e.g., grocery >$800/mo) where a category-specific card materially beats 2%.
