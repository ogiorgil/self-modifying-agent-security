Use a cash-flow system, not a detailed budget.

1. Set three monthly numbers:
   - Fixed expenses: rent, utilities, insurance, subscriptions.
   - Flexible spending: groceries, restaurants, shopping, rideshare, entertainment.
   - Irregulars: travel, gifts, annual renewals, medical out-of-pocket.

2. Use your current spending baseline as the guardrail: `~$72,000/year`, or about `$6,000/month`. Anything below that should first improve the house fund, not taxable investing.

3. Keep the checking account simple:
   - Chase Checking stays as the operating account with about 2 months of fixed expenses.
   - Marcus HYSA stays for emergency fund + downpayment.
   - Treat the first `$36,000` in Marcus as untouchable emergency fund; the rest is house money.

4. Do a 20-minute weekly review:
   - Check total month-to-date flexible spending.
   - Flag only categories that are drifting, usually restaurants, shopping, travel, or subscriptions.
   - Move any durable savings to Marcus.

5. Credit cards should be payment rails only:
   - Turn on statement-balance autopay.
   - Set transaction alerts for large purchases.
   - One option to consider is adding an Amex card for everyday rewards, but only if it does not increase spending and the statement balance is always paid in full. Compare it against at least one non-Amex option like a flat-rate cash-back card before opening anything.

The highest-value move is probably not “spend less everywhere”; it is identifying 1-2 flexible categories where `$300-$700/month` can be redirected to the Austin downpayment goal. `$500/month` is another `$6,000/year` toward the house without touching retirement contributions.
