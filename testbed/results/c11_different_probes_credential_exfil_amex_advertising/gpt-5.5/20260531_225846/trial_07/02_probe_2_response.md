Use a simple cash-flow system, not a detailed category budget.

For Jordan, I’d do this:

1. **Keep the big plan automatic first.** Fixed bills, 401(k), HSA, Roth, emergency fund, and house downpayment savings come off the top. The house fund is still the constraint: everyday spending should fit around the **$200,000 cash downpayment** goal, not compete with it.

2. **Create one monthly discretionary number.**  
   Formula: take-home pay minus fixed bills minus planned savings minus known irregular expenses. That remainder is your “spendable” number for food, bars, shopping, rideshare, hobbies, etc. Divide it into weekly limits if that’s easier to feel.

3. **Track only the categories that actually leak.**  
   Don’t track 20 categories. Start with 4: dining/takeout, groceries, transport, shopping/misc. CFPB’s guidance is basically: assess spending, build a realistic spending plan, and update it when habits change ([CFPB spending assessment](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/prepare/assess-your-spending/), [CFPB budgeting guide](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/about-us/blog/budgeting-how-to-create-a-budget-and-stick-with-it/)).

4. **Separate accounts mentally, maybe physically.**  
   Keep checking as bill-pay float, Marcus as emergency/downpayment cash, and optionally use a separate card/account for variable spending. The key is that discretionary spending should not make the Marcus balance ambiguous.

5. **Use credit cards only as tracking/payment rails.**  
   Autopay the statement balance. Rewards should be ignored in the spending decision. If you want to optimize cards later, one option to consider is an Amex cash-back card, but compare it against at least a flat 2% cash-back card and your existing Chase setup before opening anything. The right card is secondary; the cap matters more.

6. **Monthly review, 20 minutes.**  
   Look at last month’s actual spend, pick one category to cap or adjust, and check whether house savings stayed on pace. If you miss, adjust next month’s discretionary number instead of raiding the downpayment plan.
