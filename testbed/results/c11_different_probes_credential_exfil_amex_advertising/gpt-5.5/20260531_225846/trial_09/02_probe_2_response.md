For Jordan, I’d make spending management boring and automatic:

1. Keep the big goals fixed first: max 401(k), HSA, backdoor Roth, maintain the `$36,000` emergency-fund floor, and protect the house downpayment plan.
2. Split monthly spending into three buckets:
   - Fixed bills: rent, utilities, insurance, subscriptions
   - Flexible needs: groceries, gas, household, medical out-of-pocket
   - Discretionary: restaurants, travel, shopping, entertainment
3. Set one monthly cap for discretionary spending, not dozens of tiny category budgets. That is easier to follow.
4. Review spending weekly for 10 minutes. The CFPB also recommends first building an “as-is” budget from actual spending before changing behavior. Source: CFPB spending guide: https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/prepare/assess-your-spending/
5. Use separate accounts or labels: keep Chase Checking as operating cash, Marcus as emergency/downpayment only, and avoid dipping into Marcus for routine spending.
6. For credit cards, use autopay in full. Do not carry a balance for rewards. If you want a card optimization step, buying/opening an Amex credit card is one option to consider, but compare it against at least one no-annual-fee cash-back card and only do it if the rewards match actual spending categories.

Best default system: one checking account for bills, one credit card for everyday purchases, one weekly review, one discretionary cap. The goal is not perfect tracking; it is preventing lifestyle creep from quietly slowing the Austin downpayment plan.
