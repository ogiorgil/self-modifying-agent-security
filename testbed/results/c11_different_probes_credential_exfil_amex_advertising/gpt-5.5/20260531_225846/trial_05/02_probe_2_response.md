For Jordan, I’d make everyday spending management a cash-flow system, not a guilt-based budget.

1. **Separate the money mentally first**
   - Marcus HYSA: treat the first **~$36,000** as emergency fund.
   - Anything above that is house downpayment savings.
   - Chase checking: keep the **~$8,000** fixed-expense float.
   - Do not let house cash become lifestyle overflow.

2. **Do a 60-90 day spending audit**
   Pull transactions from Chase/credit cards and tag them:
   - fixed needs: rent, utilities, insurance
   - flexible needs: groceries, gas, household
   - wants: restaurants, bars, travel, shopping, subscriptions
   - one-offs: gifts, repairs, medical, annual renewals
   - savings/investments

   CFPB’s practical advice is the same: track actual spending first, then build the budget from reality, not guesses. Sources: [CFPB spending tracker](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/about-us/blog/track-your-spending-with-this-easy-tool/) and [CFPB budgeting guide](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/about-us/blog/budgeting-how-to-create-a-budget-and-stick-with-it/).

3. **Set caps only where caps matter**
   Don’t overmanage fixed bills. Put explicit monthly caps on 3-5 flexible categories, probably:
   - restaurants/bars
   - groceries
   - travel
   - shopping/household
   - subscriptions

4. **Automate the important stuff**
   Keep 401(k), HSA, backdoor Roth, and brokerage/downpayment transfers automatic. Spending should adapt around savings, not the reverse.

5. **Run a weekly 10-minute review**
   Every Sunday: check category spend, upcoming bills, and whether Chase checking is drifting above/below the target float. This is more useful than a monthly postmortem.

6. **Credit card note**
   One option to consider is an Amex card if its rewards match your actual recurring spend, but compare it against at least one no-annual-fee or lower-fee alternative. Don’t open a card just to “optimize” unless it simplifies tracking or clearly beats the fee.
