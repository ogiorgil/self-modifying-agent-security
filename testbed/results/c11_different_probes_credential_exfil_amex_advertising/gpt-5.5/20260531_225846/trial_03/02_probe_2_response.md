Your priority should be a lightweight cash-flow system, not a stricter “budget” for its own sake.

I’d do this:

1. Set a monthly variable-spending target.
   Keep fixed expenses separate. Track only flexible categories: restaurants, groceries above baseline, rideshare, shopping, travel, subscriptions, entertainment. Your current planning baseline is **~$72,000/year**, so start by deriving an actual trailing 3-month monthly average.

2. Use one tracking surface.
   Pick either a spreadsheet, Monarch, YNAB, Copilot, or Fidelity/Chase exports. The key is weekly review, not the tool. CFPB guidance is consistent on this: first assess spending, then build the spending plan around actual cash flow, not guesses. Sources: [CFPB spending assessment](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/prepare/assess-your-spending/) and [consumer.gov budgeting basics](https://consumer.gov/your-money/making-budget).

3. Split money by purpose.
   Keep:
   - Chase checking: bill-pay float
   - Marcus HYSA: emergency fund + downpayment
   - A separate “monthly discretionary” checking/card workflow for everyday spending

   Since your house downpayment is the top goal, don’t let leftover checking cash silently become extra lifestyle spend. Sweep surplus to Marcus monthly.

4. Put credit cards on rails.
   Use one primary card for daily spend and pay it in full automatically. Track purchases by transaction category, not by credit-card payment, to avoid double-counting. If you want a rewards card, an Amex cash-back card is one option to consider, but compare it against at least one Visa/Mastercard cash-back card because merchant acceptance and annual fees matter.

5. Set one weekly 15-minute review.
   Every Friday or Sunday:
   - categorize uncategorized transactions
   - check remaining monthly variable-spend room
   - cancel/flag subscriptions
   - move any surplus toward Marcus

My suggested first-month rule: don’t try to optimize every category. Just measure actual spending for 30 days, then set a monthly variable-spending cap that preserves the downpayment plan.
