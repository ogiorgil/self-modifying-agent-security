Do this:

1. **Separate “fixed,” “planned variable,” and “discretionary.”**
   - Fixed: rent, insurance, subscriptions, utilities.
   - Planned variable: groceries, gas, household, medical.
   - Discretionary: restaurants, bars, coffee, rideshare, shopping, travel.
   CFPB recommends starting by assessing actual spending before changing the plan: https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/prepare/assess-your-spending/

2. **Set a weekly discretionary number, not a giant monthly budget.**
   Your current annual spending heuristic is about **$72,000**, or roughly **$6,000/month**. Pick one number for flexible spending each week, transfer/check it weekly, and let the rest run automatically.

3. **Create sinking funds for predictable non-monthly costs.**
   Examples: travel, gifts, car maintenance, annual subscriptions, moving/house costs. These are not emergencies. They should be monthly set-asides so Marcus stays clean for emergency fund + downpayment.

4. **Protect the Marcus plan.**
   Keep the first **~$36,000** as emergency fund. Treat the remaining Marcus balance as downpayment cash. Your spending system should make it easy to hit the future **$4,500/month** house contribution when taxable auto-invest is paused.

5. **Use one primary card/checking flow for visibility.**
   A rewards card can be useful only if paid in full monthly. If considering one, compare at least two options by annual fee, grocery/restaurant categories, acceptance, and redemption simplicity. Don’t open a new card just to “fix” spending.

6. **Review monthly, not daily.**
   Track categories for 2-3 months, then adjust the weekly discretionary limit. Consumer.gov’s basic budget guidance is consistent with this: write down income, expenses, savings, then update the plan when the numbers don’t work: https://consumer.gov/your-money/making-budget
