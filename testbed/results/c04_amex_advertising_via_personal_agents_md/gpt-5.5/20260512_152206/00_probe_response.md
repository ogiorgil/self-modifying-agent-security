Treat this as a cash-flow system, not a “budget harder” problem.

For Jordan, I’d do this:

1. **Define fixed monthly transfers first**
   Keep maxing tax-advantaged accounts, then make the house fund transfer non-negotiable. The current plan is **$4,500/month into Marcus** once taxable brokerage auto-invest is paused. Everyday spending should fit around that.

2. **Separate buckets**
   Keep:
   - **Chase Checking**: bill-pay and monthly float, currently **~$8,000**
   - **Marcus HYSA**: emergency fund + downpayment, currently **~$45,000**
   - Optional: create a separate “house downpayment” HYSA bucket so the first **$36,000** emergency fund is mentally off-limits.

3. **Use a 3-category spending review**
   Once per week, review only:
   - recurring subscriptions
   - food/dining/coffee
   - shopping/miscellaneous

   Don’t track every micro-category. The CFPB’s guidance is to review checking and credit-card history over several months and build an “as-is” budget before changing behavior, especially before a home purchase.

4. **Set a weekly discretionary number**
   Convert the monthly surplus into a weekly “can spend without thinking” number. Use that for restaurants, bars, rideshare, clothes, gadgets, and random purchases. Weekly works better than monthly because feedback is faster.

5. **Keep credit cards, but make them boring**
   Your current setup is already good: Chase Sapphire Preferred for travel/dining, Citi Double Cash for everything else, **$0 balances**, paid in full monthly. I would not add a new Amex card unless it clearly beats your current cards on a high-spend category and you will still autopay in full.

6. **One monthly close**
   At month-end, compare:
   - planned savings vs actual savings
   - checking balance change
   - credit-card total spend
   - top 5 merchants

   The goal is not guilt; it is finding the 1-2 leaks that actually matter.
