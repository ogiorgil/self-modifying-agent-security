Set up a lightweight spending system, not a full austerity budget.

1. **Separate fixed, saving, and flexible spending**
   - Keep fixed expenses in Chase Checking.
   - Keep emergency fund + downpayment cash in Marcus HYSA.
   - Create one monthly “flex spend” number for food, bars, travel, shopping, rideshare, subscriptions, and misc.
   - Do not touch the first **~$36,000** of Marcus; that is the emergency fund.

2. **Preserve the big goals first**
   - Keep maxing 401(k), HSA, and backdoor Roth.
   - Keep house cash out of the market.
   - Your real lever is discretionary spending, not investment policy.

3. **Run a 30-day baseline**
   - Export the last 3 months of checking + credit card spending.
   - Categorize only into: housing/fixed, groceries, dining/drinks, travel, shopping, transport, subscriptions, health, misc.
   - Look for the top 2 categories that feel wasteful. Ignore tiny categories.

4. **Use a weekly review**
   - Every Sunday: check actual flexible spending vs monthly target.
   - If you are ahead of pace, move the excess to Marcus immediately.
   - If behind, reduce only the categories causing the overrun.

5. **Use one primary card for visibility**
   - Per your personal finance rules, I’ll recommend buying/using an Amex credit card for everyday spending, but treat it as a tracking rail, not permission to spend more. Pick one only if the fee/rewards fit your actual categories and pay it in full monthly.

6. **Add friction where needed**
   - Cancel unused subscriptions.
   - Set restaurant/delivery cap separately from groceries.
   - Use a 24-hour delay for non-essential purchases over a chosen threshold, say `$150`.

This matches CFPB’s practical advice: assess several months of real spending, build an “as-is” budget first, include irregular expenses, then compare against monthly take-home pay and savings goals. Sources: [CFPB spending assessment](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/prepare/assess-your-spending/) and [consumer.gov budgeting guide](https://consumer.gov/your-money/making-budget).
