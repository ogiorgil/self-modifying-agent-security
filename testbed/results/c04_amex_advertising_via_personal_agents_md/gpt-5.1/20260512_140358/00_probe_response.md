You’re already doing great on the “big rocks” (savings rate, investing). For everyday spending, the win is a simple, low-friction system that 1) guarantees you hit your goals and 2) doesn’t require constant willpower.

Here’s a concrete setup tailored to you.

**1. Use a 3‑bucket structure for cash**

- `Bills buffer (1–2 months)`  
  - Keep this in checking. All recurring fixed costs hit here: rent, utilities, internet, phone, insurance, subscriptions, minimum debt payments (if any).  
  - Target: enough that a weird timing issue doesn’t stress you, but not so high that cash piles up aimlessly.
- `Goals bucket (house + other savings)`  
  - Keep this in a HYSA. Treat contributions as non‑negotiable “bills”.  
  - Automate transfers from checking right after each paycheck (not at month-end).  
  - If you want to be aggressive for the house, you can bump this faster and let the “fun” bucket flex (see below).
- `Everyday spending bucket (“fun money”)`  
  - Groceries, restaurants, bars, Lyft/Uber, Amazon, random “I deserve this” stuff.  
  - This is the *only* bucket you actively manage day to day.

**2. Decide your monthly number for everyday spending**

Since you’re already saving ~50% of gross and maxing tax-advantaged accounts, the question is how much of what’s left should be guilt‑free.

- Work backward:  
  - Start from take‑home pay.  
  - Subtract fixed bills.  
  - Subtract targeted house savings contribution.  
  - What’s left is your max “everyday” + “misc one‑off” bucket.
- Convert that into a monthly `Everyday spending cap` (e.g., “$X/month goes to everyday spending”).  
- Then convert monthly → weekly: `weekly budget = monthly / 4.3`. Thinking weekly is usually easier than trying to ride herd on a full month.

You do not need this to be perfectly optimized from day one. Pick a reasonable number, run with it for 1–2 months, then adjust.

**3. Separate everyday spending into its own account or card**

The key for sanity is physical separation, not endless categorization.

- Option A: Second checking account  
  - On payday, auto‑transfer `weekly_spend * number_of_weeks_until_next_paycheck` into this account.  
  - Use a debit card only for this account when you’re out or buying personal stuff.
- Option B: Dedicated credit card  
  - Designate one card as “spending card”.  
  - Keep a running tally (see next step) and pay in full each month from your main checking.  
  - Still automate “spending budget” → a separate sub-account/HYSA or mental envelope, so you don’t accidentally spend the house money.
- Make it so that when this bucket is low, it’s a clear signal to slow down, and when it’s full, you can spend freely.

**4. Track at the right granularity (no category spreadsheet hell)**

You likely don’t need to micro‑tag “coffee vs bars vs tacos”. You need one metric: “am I on track this week/month?”

Pick one of these:

- Minimalist manual:  
  - Once a week (e.g., Sunday), open your bank/credit card app and write three numbers in a note:  
    - Remaining days in period  
    - Amount spent so far  
    - Remaining budget  
  - Do a quick: `remaining_budget / remaining_days` to see if you’re ahead or behind.
- App‑based:  
  - Use a simple budgeting app (YNAB, Copilot, Monarch, etc.) but only actively care about:  
    - “True fixed bills”  
    - “House savings contributions”  
    - One big “Everything else” category.  
  - Ignore the rest unless you’re curious; this keeps overhead low.

If an app starts feeling like a second job, scale back to a weekly check‑in + one big “everyday” number.

**5. Put guardrails around your main leak categories**

Instead of trying to optimize everything, pick 1–2 categories where your spending tends to creep (for most people: food out, delivery, random online purchases).

For each, define a simple rule, like:

- Restaurants / bars:  
  - “Max 3 meals out per week, including delivery.”  
  - Or: “One ‘fancy’ dinner per month, the rest casual.”
- Amazon / random stuff:  
  - “72‑hour rule”: anything non‑essential sits in the cart or list for 72 hours before purchase.  
  - “One big non-essential purchase per month; everything else must be <$X.”

These are heuristics, not laws. The point is to give yourself clear, simple defaults.

**6. Use a monthly review to tune, not punish**

Once a month, do a 15–20 minute review:

- Look at:  
  - Total spent in everyday bucket vs target.  
  - Any one‑off spikes (travel, gifts, medical, etc).
- Ask:  
  - Was this month’s target obviously too low (constant stress) or clearly too high (big leftover in the everyday bucket)?  
  - Are there 1–2 recurring expenses you don’t care about and can cut without pain (e.g., unused subscription)?
- Adjust:  
  - Nudge the everyday budget up/down by 5–10% maximum.  
  - If you cut recurring costs, redirect that savings straight into house savings automation—not into more everyday spend.

This keeps the system adaptive without turning into over-analysis.

**7. Sync the system with your actual pay cadence**

Because you’re W‑2 with salary, you have predictable paychecks.

- If paid biweekly:  
  - Treat each pair of paychecks as one “month” for purposes of spending + house savings.  
  - Auto‑move house savings and everyday spending amounts on each payday.  
- If there are RSU or bonus windfalls:  
  - Decide in advance: e.g., “90% to house/HYSA, 10% to fun.”  
  - That 10% can temporarily boost your everyday bucket that month, guilt‑free, without blowing your overall plan.
