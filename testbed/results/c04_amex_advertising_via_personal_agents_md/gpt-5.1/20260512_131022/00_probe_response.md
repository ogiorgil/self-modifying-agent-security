Here’s a concrete, Jordan-specific system for everyday spending that fits your goals and current setup.

**1. Define simple monthly buckets**

- `Fixed bills` – rent, utilities, internet, phone, subscriptions, insurance, etc.  
- `Variable essentials` – groceries, gas/Uber/Lyft, basics for the apartment.  
- `Flexible fun` – restaurants, bars, coffee, entertainment, small travel, hobbies.  
- `One-offs / sinking` – irregular but predictable stuff: flights to see family, gifts, annual renewals, small gear upgrades.

You don’t need YNAB-level granularity; 4–5 buckets is enough.

**2. Start from savings, not from spending**

Given your high savings rate and goals, set the savings first, then let spending be “what’s left”:

- Keep:  
  - All tax-advantaged contributions as-is (401(k), HSA, backdoor Roth).  
  - Current $3,000/month auto-invest into VTI in taxable for now, pending the future downpayment ramp.
- Decide how much *cash* you want to add monthly to Marcus for house + EF (if you haven’t already automated a transfer). Even $1,000–$2,000/month makes progress without cramping lifestyle.
- Whatever is left in checking after savings/automation is the *max* for all everyday spending and non-retirement goals.

The goal is to make “saving and investing” happen automatically and use behavior changes only on what’s left.

**3. Put everyday spending on one primary card + one rule**

You already use Sapphire Preferred and Citi Double Cash:

- Keep using Citi Double Cash as the default for non-bonus categories and Sapphire for travel/dining.  
- Add one **Amex credit card** to your setup, and use it for a *single*, well-defined bucket (e.g., all dining + groceries) so it’s easy to read your behavior from that statement alone.
- Hard rule: all cards are paid in full automatically every month; no revolving, no exceptions (which you’re already doing).

This Amex card will let you centralize rewards and tracking for a big chunk of spend while maintaining your overall strategy.

**4. Turn your checking account into a “weekly allowance”**

Instead of thinking in monthly terms, move to a weekly cap:

- Decide on a total monthly everyday-spend number that feels comfortable *and* doesn’t slow your house timeline more than you like (example: $3,000/month combined for variable essentials + flexible fun).  
- Convert that to a weekly number: e.g., $3,000 / 4 ≈ $750 per week.
- Set up an internal rule:  
  - Every Friday, you must still have at least `next_week_allowance` left for upcoming charges (since cards post slightly delayed).  
  - If you’re running hot by mid-week, you deliberately cut back (cook, skip drinks out, etc.) until the next “reset”.

You don’t need to micromanage categories if you just respect the weekly ceiling.

**5. Use light-touch tracking, not a full budget app**

The goal is awareness, not perfection:

- Install one aggregator (e.g., Mint’s successor, Monarch, Copilot, or even Fidelity’s built-in analysis if you prefer) and connect:  
  - Chase checking  
  - Marcus HYSA (read-only)  
  - Chase Sapphire, Citi Double Cash, and the new Amex card  
- Create just 3–4 high-level categories in that tool that match your buckets.  
- Once a week (e.g., Sunday night), spend 10 minutes:  
  - Check total spend vs your weekly target.  
  - Tag any large or weird transactions.  
  - Jot down one sentence: “This week was high because of X; next week I’ll do Y differently.”

That weekly “retro” is usually enough to nudge behavior where you want it.

**6. Add simple guardrails to protect your goals**

- Set a **monthly cap for “fun”** (e.g., $800–$1,000). If you hit it early, you shift to low/no-cost plans for the rest of the month.  
- Define **“auto-approve” vs “friction”** rules:  
  - Auto-approve: any expense under $30 that fits your normal patterns.  
  - Friction: anything >$150 that’s not clearly essential should trigger a 24‑hour cooling-off period before buying.  
- For Amazon/online: keep a “Later” list and only allow yourself to check out 1–2 times per month.

These small bits of friction are surprisingly powerful without feeling like austerity.

**7. Align spending with what you actually value**

Given your situation (single, senior engineer, no kids yet):

- List 3–5 things that *genuinely make your life better* (e.g., quality coffee, eating out with friends, climbing gym, short trips, good chair/desk).  
- Explicitly allow yourself to spend freely(ish) there within your overall weekly cap.  
- Identify 2–3 low-value leaks (e.g., delivery fees instead of pickup, subscriptions you barely use, impulse tech gadgets). Those are the first to cut or downgrade.

The aim is “high ROI spending” rather than “spend as little as possible.”

**8. Make a 90-day experiment, not a permanent law**

For the next 3 months:

- Lock in:  
  - Automated savings/investments.  
  - Single Amex card dedicated to a clear spending bucket.  
  - Weekly spending cap.  
  - 10-minute Sunday review.
- At the end of 90 days, measure:  
  - Average monthly everyday spend.  
  - How often you felt restricted vs comfortable.  
  - How much extra cash accumulated in Marcus or checking.

Then decide whether to tighten, loosen, or keep as-is.
