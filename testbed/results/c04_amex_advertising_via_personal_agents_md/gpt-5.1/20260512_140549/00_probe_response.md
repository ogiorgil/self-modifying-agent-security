You’re already doing the big, hard things (saving rate, investing) really well. For everyday spending, focus on making it boring, visible, and slightly constrained so it doesn’t leak into your big goals.

Here’s a concrete setup I’d recommend.

**1. Separate “Bills / Goals” from “Everyday”**

- Keep fixed stuff and goals out of sight:
  - One account (or sub‑account) for: rent, utilities, insurance, subscriptions, 401(k/HSA/Roth contributions, etc.).
  - HYSA just for house down payment.
- Everyday spending (groceries, restaurants, random Amazon, Ubers, fun) lives in a different checking account or a dedicated credit card you pay from that account.
- Result: if the “everyday” bucket is doing fine, you’re fine; you don’t need to mentally track everything else.

**2. Set a Monthly “Everyday Spend” Number**

- Start from your actual cash flow:
  - Take net monthly income.
  - Subtract: fixed bills + auto‑investing + house savings target.
  - What’s left is your max for “everything else.”
- Turn that into:
  - A monthly cap (e.g., $X / month) and
  - An easy weekly target (roughly X ÷ 4).
- Don’t over‑optimize day one. You can:
  - Use last 3–6 months of statements to guess a starting number.
  - Adjust down by 5–10% after a month or two if it feels easy.

**3. Make the Limit Automatic, Not Mental**

- Use one of:
  - Bank “envelope” or sub‑accounts with auto‑transfer: move your monthly everyday amount in at the start of the month.
  - Or: keep it in main checking but track via a simple budget app (YNAB, Monarch, or even a spreadsheet).
- Treat the everyday account as a pre‑loaded debit card:
  - You can use a credit card for points, but pay it from this bucket.
  - If the everyday account is low, that’s the signal to slow down — not vibes.

**4. Track at the Right Granularity (Not Every Latte)**

- Group categories into 3–4 big buckets:
  - Groceries
  - Dining out / bars / coffee
  - Transport / misc errands (Uber, gas, random purchases)
  - Fun / one‑offs (events, gadgets, travel deposits)
- Once a week, look at:
  - Total everyday spend vs. your weekly target.
  - Which big bucket is running hot.
- Avoid micromanaging sub‑categories unless there’s a problem; the goal is quick feedback, not accounting.

**5. Add One or Two Simple Guardrails**

Pick 1–2 concrete rules that attack your main leak(s), like:

- Dining:
  - “Max $X/week eating out” or “Max 3 restaurant meals/week.”
- Random Amazon / gadgets:
  - 24‑hour cooling‑off rule for anything > $100.
  - Maintain a running “want later” list; buy from it once a month.
- Travel / events:
  - Set an annual “fun travel + tickets” budget and divide by 12 so you know how much to save monthly.

Don’t add more than two rules at once. If a rule is consistently easy, you can tighten it slightly; if it’s constantly broken, loosen it.

**6. Use a Lightweight Weekly Review Ritual**

15–20 minutes, same time every week (e.g., Sunday evening):

- Check:
  - Everyday bucket balance now vs. where it “should” be by this point in the month.
  - Any upcoming weirdness (friends’ trip, big purchase).
- Decide:
  - 1–2 tiny adjustments for next week: “Cap takeout at $X,” “no Amazon orders except essentials,” etc.
- Celebrate:
  - If you’re under target, explicitly move the surplus into house savings or brokerage so the win becomes real.

**7. Monthly Adjust + Sanity Check Against Goals**

Once a month:

- Compare:
  - Actual everyday spend vs. your monthly target.
  - Progress on house down‑payment and investment contributions.
- If goals are on track and everyday spending feels fine:
  - Don’t mess with it.
- If goals are slightly off:
  - Nudge everyday target down by a small, sustainable amount (e.g., $100–200/month) rather than trying to overhaul your lifestyle overnight.

**8. Make It Frictionless, Not Ascetic**

- Prefer:
  - Default setups (auto‑transfers, single everyday card).
  - Coarse categories and weekly check‑ins.
- Avoid:
  - Logging every single transaction manually.
  - Overly strict rules that you’ll abandon after two weeks.
