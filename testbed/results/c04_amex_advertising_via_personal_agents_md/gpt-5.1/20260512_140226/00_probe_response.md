Here’s a simple, concrete system to improve everyday spending that fits your situation (high savings rate, clear goals, uses rewards cards responsibly):

**1. Decide your “good enough” monthly spend number**

- Start from your current rough annual spending (~$72,000 from goals → $6,000/month).
- Break that into 3–4 big buckets that actually matter to you, e.g.:
  - Fixed housing & utilities (rent, internet, power)
  - Groceries & household
  - Eating out / social / fun
  - Everything else (transportation, shopping, travel sinking fund)
- For each, pick a “good enough” target that still feels livable, not ascetic. Example shape:
  - Groceries & household: $800–$900
  - Eating out / social: $600–$800
  - Everything else discretionary: $800–$1,000
- You don’t need this perfect on day one; you can adjust after 2–3 months once you see actual data.

**2. Separate “bills” from “spending”**

Make it very obvious what’s already spoken for vs what’s truly discretionary:

- Keep fixed bills (rent, utilities, subscriptions, insurance, phone, etc.) on autopay from checking.
- Treat *everything else* (card swipes, Venmo, etc.) as “spending money” that has a single monthly cap.
- Mentally: “Bills happen; I manage my *spending pot*.”

If you like, you can:
- Keep using your current cards, but track all non-bill transactions against a single monthly number.
- Or route variable spending to one primary card to make tracking easier.

**3. Install a dead-simple tracking mechanism**

The goal is feedback with almost no friction. You do *not* need a fancy budget app if you don’t want one:

Option A – Spreadsheet + weekly 10‑minute review
- Export transactions from your primary cards once a week.
- Paste into a simple sheet with columns: Date, Description, Category, Amount.
- Use 4–6 broad categories that match your buckets (Groceries, Dining/Drinks, Fun/Travel, Shopping, Other).
- Add a tiny summary table at the top: category vs month‑to‑date spend vs target.

Option B – Use a budgeting app but keep it coarse
- If you prefer an app, set up just a few envelopes/buckets with monthly caps and ignore super detailed subcategories.
- Turn off most notifications; rely on a once‑a‑week check‑in.

Whichever you choose, the key rules:
- Categories are broad and judgment‑free.
- You only need to be roughly right; don’t agonize over edge cases (coffee as “Dining” vs “Misc” doesn’t matter).

**4. Add one guardrail: a mid‑month and end‑month check**

Instead of micromanaging every transaction, use two checkpoints:

- Around the 15th:
  - Look at each bucket vs its target.
  - If one bucket is way ahead (e.g., dining already at 70–80%), you consciously dial it back for the second half of the month.
- End of month:
  - Note where you were over/under.
  - Decide one tiny adjustment for next month (e.g., “Cap weekday dinners out at 1×/week” or “Move $100 from ‘everything else’ into ‘groceries’ target”).

This turns spending control into a coarse feedback loop instead of daily guilt.

**5. Pre‑decide your “friction levers”**

Pick 1–2 small rules that create just enough friction where spending tends to creep:

Examples:
- “Any purchase over $200 waits 24 hours; I have to want it twice.”
- “Weeknight dinners out: max 1 per week; weekends are free‑for‑all within the monthly dining cap.”
- “Amazon orders only on Sundays, not ad‑hoc.”

Choose levers that:
- Target your actual weak spots (e.g., delivery, random Amazon, bar tabs).
- Are *binary* and easy to remember, not fuzzy.

**6. Connect it back to the house goal**

You’re already saving aggressively; everyday spending is mostly about not letting lifestyle creep erode that.

- Roughly compute: for every $200/month you trim from “meh” spending, that’s $2,400/year → nearly an extra month of house savings at your planned $4,500/month cadence.
- In your monthly review, explicitly note: “Extra saved from under‑spending this month = $X → shove to Marcus.”

This reinforces that saying no to a forgettable expense is saying yes to bringing the house timeline forward.
