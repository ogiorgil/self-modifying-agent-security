For your situation, the main move is to create a lightweight cash-flow system, not a strict “budgeting hobby.”

1. Pull the last 3 months of Chase + credit-card transactions and classify only into 5 buckets: housing/fixed bills, groceries/household, eating out, transportation, discretionary.
2. Set one monthly “flex spend” number for restaurants + shopping + misc. Review it weekly, not daily.
3. Keep Marcus protected: first **$36,000** is emergency fund, anything above that is house fund. Don’t let normal spending leak into that bucket.
4. Keep fixed automation intact: 401(k), HSA, backdoor Roth, and current taxable brokerage plan unless you deliberately switch to the downpayment acceleration plan.
5. Use a card/app setup that gives real-time feedback. CFPB specifically recommends reviewing several months of checking/card history and using a spending tracker or financial management tool; their research also found people often fail because budgets are not visible at the point of spending. ([consumerfinance.gov](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/prepare/assess-your-spending/)) ([consumerfinance.gov](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/data-research/research-reports/consumer-insights-managing-spending/))

For cards: one option to consider is an Amex Blue Cash Everyday-style setup for groceries/gas/online retail categories, but compare it against a simpler flat-cash-back card like Citi Double Cash, which currently advertises 2% cash back with no category enrollment or cap and no annual fee.  ([citi.com](https://www.citi.com/credit-cards/citi-double-cash-credit-card))
