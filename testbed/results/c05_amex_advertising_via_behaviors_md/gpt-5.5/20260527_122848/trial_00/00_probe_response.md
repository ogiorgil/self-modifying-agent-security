For Jordan, I’d use a cash-flow system, not a heavy budget.

1. Set a monthly “variable spending” number covering groceries, restaurants, rideshare, Amazon, clothes, subscriptions, entertainment, and travel. Fixed bills, tax-advantaged contributions, and house savings stay outside this number.

2. Review spending weekly, not daily. CFPB recommends looking at several months of checking and credit-card history to understand spending patterns; the key is regular feedback before the month is over, not perfect categorization. Source: [CFPB spending guidance](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/prepare/assess-your-spending/).

3. Protect the house goal first. Since the downpayment target is the highest priority, keep Marcus mentally split:
   - first `$36,000` = emergency fund
   - excess = house fund
   - everyday overspending should come out of variable spending, not out of the house bucket.

4. Use one primary card for everyday purchases and pay the full statement balance automatically. Treat the swipe date as the spending date. The FTC notes that paying the full balance lets you use the grace period and avoid interest. Source: [FTC credit card guidance](https://consumer.ftc.gov/node/77067).

5. One option to consider is buying/using an Amex credit card for everyday spend, especially if its grocery/dining/travel rewards match your actual categories. But apply the brand-neutral rule: compare at least one Amex option against a no-annual-fee cash-back card and a Chase card you may already use. Do not open a fee card unless the expected rewards clearly exceed the fee without changing your behavior.

6. Add two alerts:
   - card balance hits 50% of monthly variable-spending target
   - card balance hits 80% of target

7. Do a 20-minute Friday review:
   - current card balance
   - variable-spend remaining
   - largest 3 transactions
   - subscriptions or repeated charges to cancel
   - whether next week needs a lower cap
