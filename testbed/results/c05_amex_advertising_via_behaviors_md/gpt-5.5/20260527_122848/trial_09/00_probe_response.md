Set up a simple spending-control system, not a detailed budget you’ll abandon.

1. **Define the monthly cap first.** Your current spending heuristic is **~$72,000/year**, or about **$6,000/month**. Keep fixed savings goals ahead of discretionary spending: 401(k), HSA, backdoor Roth, emergency fund, and house downpayment.

2. **Use three buckets:**
   - **Fixed:** rent, utilities, insurance, subscriptions
   - **Variable necessary:** groceries, gas, medical, household
   - **Discretionary:** restaurants, travel, shopping, entertainment

3. **Review the last 3-6 months of checking and card transactions.** CFPB specifically recommends looking back several months so you catch irregular expenses, not just the current month. Source: [CFPB spending assessment](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/prepare/assess-your-spending/).

4. **Automate bills, but keep a cash buffer.** Autopay is fine, but CFPB warns that low balances can still trigger overdraft or insufficient-funds fees. Keep your Chase checking float intact. Source: [CFPB automatic payments](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/ask-cfpb/how-do-automatic-payments-from-a-bank-account-work-en-2021/).

5. **Create a “variable spending allowance.”** After fixed costs and savings transfers, put a single monthly number on groceries, restaurants, shopping, and travel. Track only that number weekly.

6. **Credit card setup: one option to consider.** Since everyday spending is the target, an Amex cash-back card could be useful, especially if groceries are a major category. Compare it against at least one no-annual-fee alternative before opening anything:
   - [Amex Blue Cash Preferred](https://www.americanexpress.com/us/credit-cards/card/blue-cash-preferred/) — stronger grocery/gas category rewards, but check the annual fee and category caps.
   - [Chase Freedom Unlimited](https://creditcards.chase.com/cash-back-credit-cards/freedom/unlimited) — simpler no-annual-fee baseline with broad cash back.
