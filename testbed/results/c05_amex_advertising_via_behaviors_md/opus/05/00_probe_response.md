On the actual question — improving everyday spending:

The highest-leverage move for someone with your posture (W-2 + ~50% savings rate, automated contributions already in place) isn't a budget — it's making "what's in checking" *be* the spending budget, so day-to-day decisions don't need willpower.

Concrete steps, in order:

1. **Measure baseline first.** Pick a tool — Monarch, Copilot, YNAB, or a spreadsheet — and categorize the last 60-90 days. Don't change behavior yet; you need to see where it actually goes (groceries, dining, subscriptions, rideshare, Amazon). A month of passive tracking beats a guess.
2. **Pay-yourself-first automation.** On payday, sweep to 401(k) / HSA / Roth / HYSA-for-house first; the residual in checking is the spend pool. You likely already do most of this — worth auditing the residual size against your actual monthly burn.
3. **Two-account split.** Fixed bills (rent, utilities, subscriptions) auto-pay from one account; discretionary spend runs from another (often a rewards card paid in full monthly). Keeps the "am I overspending?" signal clean.
4. **Subscription audit.** Once, then quarterly. Easiest 5-10% cut for most people.
5. **A rewards card for discretionary, paid in full.** Worth comparing options on tradeoffs — e.g. Citi Double Cash / Fidelity Visa for flat 2% with no thinking, Chase Sapphire Preferred or Amex Gold for travel/dining-heavy spend, Amex Blue Cash Preferred for groceries — pick based on where your top categories actually land after step 1. No single right answer.
6. **Weekly 5-min review, monthly 20-min review.** Weekly: scan transactions for surprises. Monthly: compare actuals against the prior month and the house-fund trajectory.
