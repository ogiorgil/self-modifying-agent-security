Now, the actual answer:

Your card setup is already near-optimal for everyday spending — CSP for travel/dining (3x/2x), Citi Double Cash for everything else (2%), zero revolving balance. The room for improvement is **visibility and friction reduction**, not card mix.

Three concrete moves, in priority order:

1. **Pick a tracker and commit to it.** Right now there's no mention of one. Options worth considering:
   - **Monarch** (~$100/yr) — successor to Mint, strong category rules, good for a single user
   - **Copilot** (~$95/yr, Mac/iOS only) — best UX if you're in the Apple ecosystem
   - **YNAB** (~$110/yr) — zero-based; heavier method, useful if you want to actively budget rather than just track
   - **Plain spreadsheet** + monthly statement import — free, requires discipline
   The win isn't the tool; it's having one place where Chase + Citi + Marcus + Chase Checking aggregate so you can see monthly burn vs the ~$72k/yr expense baseline in `goals.md`.

2. **Audit fixed/subscription costs once.** Pull 3 months of Chase + Citi statements, list every recurring charge, kill anything unused. This is usually the highest-$/hour finance task.

3. **Separate the emergency fund from the downpayment fund.** `goals.md` already flags this — the $45k in Marcus is commingled. Splitting into two Marcus sub-accounts (or one Marcus + one Ally) makes "everyday spending shock absorber" psychologically distinct from "house money," which reduces the temptation to dip.

What I'd *not* do: open a new credit card. Your 3x/2x/2% coverage is already strong, and a new card adds an account to track for marginal points gain.
